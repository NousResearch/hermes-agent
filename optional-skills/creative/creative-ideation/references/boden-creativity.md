# Boden — Three Kinds of Creative Move

Margaret Boden, *The Creative Mind: Myths and Mechanisms* (1990; 2nd ed. Routledge, 2004). The theoretical spine of this skill: a way to diagnose **what kind of novelty a prompt needs**, so you route to a method that actually supplies it instead of guessing.

## Creativity = novelty + value

Mere novelty is not creativity, and not the goal. Random is novel and worthless. An idea must be both new *and* good for its situation. This is the skill's viability floor (operating rule 5), stated at the root.

## P-creative vs H-creative

- **P-creative** — new to the person who made it. The realistic, honest target.
- **H-creative** — new in all of history. Rare; don't claim it.

The anti-slop tests (swap-a-noun, one-element-needs-explaining) are P-creativity tests: did *this* prompt produce something that isn't the field's default?

## The conceptual space — and where slop lives

Every domain is a **conceptual space**: a style with rules — the set of things that already count as "a sonata," "a startup pitch," "a habit app." A language model samples the **center** of that space: the highest-probability point, the average of everything written. **That center is slop.** Three moves get you off it:

| Move | What it does | Diagnose when the prompt is… | Method family |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Combinational** | new links between existing ideas | disconnected; wants a fresh angle or an outside analogy | `analogy-and-blending` · `biomimicry` · `chance-and-remix` |
| **Exploratory** | search unvisited regions *within* the space | thin; wants variations / coverage of a known form | `scamper` · `oulipo` · `volume-generation` · `story-skeletons` · `pattern-languages` · `derive-and-mapping` · `jobs-to-be-done` · `polya` · `oblique-strategies` |
| **Transformational** | drop or alter a *defining* constraint of the space | too safe; "been done"; stuck inside an assumption | `lateral-provocations` · `pataphysics` · `first-principles` · `triz-principles` · `defamiliarization` · `leverage-points` |

Cross-cutting: **selection** (`premortem-and-inversion`, `compression-progress`) supplies the *value* half — it doesn't generate. **Practice / synthesis** (`creative-discipline`, `affinity-diagrams`) feed all three.

*Straddlers:* `oblique-strategies` and `defamiliarization` sit between exploratory and transformational depending on the card/move drawn; `triz` resolves a contradiction by changing a dimension (transformational) using a pattern library (combinational flavor). Assign by dominant effect.

## How to use it in routing

1. **Combinational is cheapest, exploratory is the workhorse, transformational is rarest and strongest** — and the one slop never reaches. Combinational ideas feel *clever*; transformational ideas feel *impossible-until-explained*. On slop terrain, aim for the second.
2. **High-slop terrain and "too safe" are transformational by default.** Exploratory variation on a slop seed stays slop: you must break a constraint, not vary within it. This is *why* the high-slop override forces `lateral-provocations` / `pataphysics`.
3. The diagnosis picks the **family**; PHASE + DOMAIN pick the method inside it.

## See also

Finke, Ward & Smith's **Geneplore** model (generate → explore) is the same two-beat rhythm this skill runs at the response level: produce candidates, then push them off-center. Use Boden to choose the *kind* of push.
